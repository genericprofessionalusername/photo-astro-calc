Standard reference value for a V-band-like passband. An assumption that sets the absolute scale. The optimal sub length scales linearly with F₀ × QE × transmission, and for a broadband colour camera under a light-polluted (emission-line) sky this product can be off by ~2× either way — treat the seconds as a planning estimate, not a precision target. Relative results (verdicts, frame-count ratios) are much less sensitive.
A sub counts as "sky-limited" once the sky signal per frame exceeds this multiple of read noise squared. At 10×, read noise adds under ~5% to total noise.
Usually set by the target picker in the frames panel. The named-object values there are derived by spreading each object's catalogued total magnitude over its catalogued angular size — a defensible average, but an approximation: real objects are much brighter in their cores than their fringes.
The frame floor exists for outlier rejection, not SNR — sigma-clip stacking needs a crowd of frames to vote out satellites, planes, and hot pixels. ~10 is comfortable, 6 mostly works, 1 means every artifact in that frame is permanent. Set it to 1 to see pure SNR-driven counts.
SNR ≈ 5 is a common threshold for a rough but real detection; past SNR ≈ 20 each added hour buys little visible improvement, because noise only falls with the square root of frame count.

Approximate averages: each object's total light spread over its full extent. Real objects are uneven — M42's core runs some 4 magnitudes brighter than its wings, so the core is done long before the frame count shown here, which budgets for the outskirts. Why focal length barely moves these numbers: for extended objects, per-pixel signal depends only on f-ratio and pixel pitch. Doubling focal length at the same f-number quadruples the light-collecting area but shrinks each pixel's patch of sky by the same factor — they cancel exactly (watch those two worksheet rows move in opposite directions). A longer lens buys detail, not per-pixel depth: it resolves the target across more pixels, and only recovers an SNR advantage if you later downsample to a common output size. Swapping lenses changes these counts through the f-number, not the millimetres. (Stars are the exception — as point sources they do gain with aperture, which this extended-object model doesn't score.)
This math assumes nothing is clipping. If stars or sky are hitting pure white in your subs, the model no longer applies — shorten the sub or lower ISO. And stacking only averages away random noise: it will not remove the light-pollution glow itself, and it cannot restore colour that skyglow has washed out. Under a bright sky, more frames buy you a smoother image of a low-contrast scene — filters or a darker site are what change the scene. Dark current (heat signal) is ignored here; on warm nights it adds noise this calculator doesn't count.

Where the preset numbers come from

Read-noise pre-fills marked verified come from published measurements of input-referred read noise (photonstophotos.net data or writeups citing it): Sony A7S 3.56 e⁻ across ISO 125–1600 dropping to ≈0.94 e⁻ from ISO 2000 up; Canon 90D 1.74 e⁻ at ISO 800, 1.45 e⁻ at ISO 1600, 1.09 e⁻ at ISO 6400; Canon R6 1.26 e⁻ at ISO 12800 and under 2 e⁻ from ISO 6400 up; Sony A7 III's dual-gain step at ISO 640. For the newer bodies: Canon R5 II 1.18 e⁻ at ISO 400 with a second gain mode from ISO 500; Sony A7 IV dual-gain step at ISO 400; Nikon Z8 high-gain conversion from ISO 500; Canon R7 sensor geometry (22.3 × 14.8 mm, 3.2 µm) from direct measurement, read noise slightly below the 90D per chart comparisons; a measured banding caveat appears only when a faint target makes it relevant. Values marked interpolated are filled in between those anchors and can easily be a few tenths of an electron off — for real planning, read your camera's curve on photonstophotos.net and type the exact number. The Bortle → mag/arcsec² table follows a published approximate mapping (Bortle 1: 21.75–22.0 … Bortle 7: 18.0–18.5; classes 8–9 below 18).

mag/arcsec² (sky brightness)
How bright one tiny patch of sky is, on the astronomers' backwards scale: higher number = darker sky. 21.9 is a pristine site; 17 is downtown.
Read noise
Random electrical error added once per frame when the sensor is read out, measured in electrons. The only noise source you pay per-frame rather than per-second.
Sky-limited
Your exposure is long enough that noise from skyglow photons, not the camera's read noise, dominates. Past this point, longer subs stop helping SNR.
SNR (signal-to-noise ratio)
Target signal divided by total noise. Higher = smoother, deeper image. Stacking N frames multiplies it by √N.
